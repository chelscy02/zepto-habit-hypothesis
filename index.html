Why I chose Zepto

I use Zepto. Most people I know use Zepto. But as a commerce and strategy student, I kept asking a question I couldn't find a rigorous answer to anywhere: are they building something real, or are they running an elaborate cash transfer from VCs to consumers?

When their DRHP dropped in December 2025, it gave me access to actual audited data for the first time. I spent several weeks going through it alongside Zomato's annual reports, Swiggy's IPO filing, and every piece of industry research I could find.

This is what I found. I've tried to be explicit about what I know, what I've estimated, and where I'm genuinely uncertain.

My starting assumption, going in, was that Zepto's discount strategy was financially reckless. Most reporting on the company frames it as a cash-burning machine that built loyalty on shaky foundations. That framing turned out to be too simple.

The DRHP contains a data point I did not expect: 45–49% of users who placed their first Zepto order in FY23 were still active on the platform three years later. That's a retention figure most consumer apps would consider excellent. It made me question my initial assumption.

But the DRHP also contains a troubling counter-signal: users acquired in FY25 show only 31% retention at the same stage of their lifecycle. Whether that's a temporary cohort effect or structural deterioration is the most important unresolved question in this entire investigation.

The five questions below represent the investigative threads I followed. Each one changed my thinking in some way. I've tried to show the reasoning, not just the conclusions.

Question 1

Can Zepto acquire customers without permanently relying on discounts?

My hypothesis going in
My initial assumption was that Zepto's growth was almost entirely discount-driven — that the company was essentially paying people to try the app, and that reducing subsidies would cause immediate churn. I expected the data to confirm this. It partially did, but the direction of travel surprised me.

The most useful metric I found for this question wasn't retention data or GMV figures. It was a single line in the DRHP: digital marketing cost per order fell from ₹10.82 in Q1 FY24 to ₹1.01 in Q4 FY26. That's a 91% decline over ten quarters.

I spent some time trying to find alternative explanations for this number before accepting it at face value. Could it be that Zepto simply shifted spend from digital performance marketing to offline channels? The total advertising spend grew from ₹304 crore in FY24 to ₹1,389 crore in FY26, so the absolute marketing budget expanded significantly — it's not that they stopped spending.

The more plausible interpretation is that a growing proportion of orders are coming from users who were not acquired via paid ads. They're returning because they've used the app before, not because they saw a promotional banner. If this is accurate, it suggests that some portion of the discount spending earlier in the company's life did result in organic return behaviour.

I attempted to separate repeat order rates from acquisition-driven orders using the DRHP data. The DRHP does not break this down by channel. I couldn't verify the organic vs. paid split directly, so the 91% cost decline remains a proxy rather than a confirmed causal signal.

I looked at this alongside the blended customer acquisition cost. Using total FY26 advertising spend (₹1,389 crore) divided across the net new annual transacting users added between FY24 and FY26 (roughly 37 million new users), the implied blended CAC is somewhere between ₹800 and ₹1,400 per acquired user. That's a wide range, and the actual figure depends heavily on what proportion of ad spend was retention-focused vs. acquisition-focused — which the DRHP doesn't specify.

The direction of evidence on Question 1 is moderately positive. Marketing cost per order has fallen dramatically, which is consistent with — but not definitive proof of — organic habit formation reducing paid acquisition dependency. The remaining uncertainty is whether this trend holds as Zepto expands into less familiar geographies.

Question 2

Do customers actually become habitual users after the discount ends?

My hypothesis going in
I expected retention data to be weak — the standard critique of discount-led acquisition is that price-sensitive customers churn immediately when incentives disappear. I assumed I would find evidence supporting this view. Instead, I found a more complicated picture.

The DRHP discloses that 45.2% to 49.8% of users who placed their first order in FY23 were still active three years later. I want to be careful about how I interpret this, because the number is genuinely surprising and I initially assumed I was misreading the DRHP.

After reviewing the relevant section multiple times, the number appears to be a real disclosure. The FY23 cohort — acquired three years ago, well before the current IPO momentum — is retaining at roughly half its original size after three years. For a grocery delivery app competing against Blinkit, Instamart, and the local kirana, that is a meaningful retention figure.

What explains it? One signal is category expansion. The DRHP reports that new users purchase from 2–3 product categories in their first month. By month 23, that number expands to around 18 categories. A user who originally downloaded Zepto for vegetables and now buys from electronics, pharmacy, and personal care on the same platform is substantially harder to replace with a competitor.

Counter-evidence I cannot ignore
The FY25 Q3 cohort showed only 31.1% retention by Q4 FY26 — materially lower than the FY23 cohort at the same lifecycle stage. The FY26 Q1 cohort was at 32% by Q3 FY26. Whether this is because newer users are more price-sensitive, because Zepto is expanding into geographies where habits form more slowly, or because competitive intensity has increased, I cannot determine from public data.

I also looked at monthly active users. The DRHP notes that Zepto's month-on-month user count in March 2026 showed a sequential decline for the first time — this is an early warning signal that sits uncomfortably alongside the positive FY23 retention numbers.

The habit thesis is supported for early cohorts. It is not yet confirmed for cohorts acquired in FY25 and FY26. The divergence between these cohorts is the most important unresolved data point in this entire project. The answer will only be visible in the data by late 2027.

Product teardown — how Zepto tries to install the habit

1
First-order onboarding — the trigger installation
+

The app opens to a location confirmation, then immediately surfaces "10 minutes" prominently. Everything about the first session is designed to anchor one association: if I need something now, Zepto solves it. The first-order cashback (typically ₹100–200) removes the price barrier to testing this claim.

The 10-minute delivery promise is also a product commitment that the company spends enormous operational cost to maintain. Every time it's fulfilled, it reinforces the core behavioural trigger. Compared to Blinkit and Instamart, Zepto has historically been more aggressive about maintaining this promise even in lower-density geographies.

Behaviour being installed: "When I need something immediately, I open Zepto before I consider alternatives."
2
Zepto Pass — converting occasional users to committed ones
+

Zepto Pass (roughly ₹100–149/month) offers free delivery and priority access. The subscription model changes the user's psychology: once someone pays a monthly fee, they feel compelled to use the service to justify the cost — economists call this a "sunk cost nudge." This is distinct from the initial discount phase: it's self-reinforcing retention, not paid retention.

The DRHP reports 40+ lakh subscribers contributing approximately ₹480 crore annually in FY25. That's a meaningful signal that some users have crossed from occasional to committed. I compared this to Blinkit's Club membership and Swiggy One — Zepto's pricing is competitive but the subscriber base appears smaller relative to their user base.

Behaviour being installed: "I've paid for Zepto Pass, so I should use it for everything I can — vegetables, snacks, phone chargers, medicines."
3
Search and discovery — basket expansion mechanism
+

I spent time observing the in-app search experience and comparing it to Blinkit. Zepto's search is notably aggressive about showing sponsored results and related category suggestions. When you search for "chips," you get chips, but also soft drinks, dips, and a branded snacks section — all of which may be sponsored placements.

This is not accidental. The app is deliberately expanding the user's basket size. Every additional category a user discovers through search is a category they previously sourced elsewhere (kirana, BigBasket, a supermarket). Converting those purchases to Zepto increases AOV, reduces competitor surface area, and contributes to the 2.6 → 18 category expansion the DRHP reports.

Behaviour being installed: "Zepto has everything I need. I don't need to go to the supermarket anymore."
4
Notifications — the re-engagement layer
+

The DRHP mentions a 15% improvement in 30-day retention from targeted re-marketing of lapsed users. Zepto's notification cadence is aggressive — I tracked approximately 8–12 push notifications per week during a recent test period. The notifications include price alerts, flash sales, and personalised product suggestions.

This is a double-edged mechanism. Re-engagement notifications prevent churn among occasional users, but they also represent continued paid engagement effort. The question is whether notifications are maintaining organically formed habits or compensating for habits that never fully formed. The 15% re-engagement improvement doesn't distinguish between these.

Behaviour being installed (or artificially sustained): "I haven't ordered in two weeks — here's a reminder with a 20% offer on something you've bought before."
Conclusion confidence — Question 2 Medium confidence — split finding
The evidence is genuinely mixed and I changed my view partway through this investigation. For FY23 cohorts, the retention data is better than I expected and the category expansion signal is credible. For FY25–26 cohorts, the early retention numbers are materially weaker. Whether this gap closes over time is the single most important open question.
Why split confidence: Two real data points pointing in opposite directions. I cannot resolve the FY25 cohort question from available public data, and I'm unwilling to speculate past what the evidence supports.
Question 3

Does advertising revenue replace subsidy economics at scale?

My hypothesis going in
I initially dismissed the advertising thesis as a convenient narrative that companies attach to themselves when their core economics don't work. Amazon has made this model real, but Amazon also had 20 years and a completely different scale. I didn't expect Zepto's ad product to be meaningful yet. I was wrong.

Amazon's advertising is ~7–8% of GMV in mature markets. Zepto is approaching this benchmark significantly faster than I anticipated. This is the finding that most changed my overall assessment.

The advertising trajectory is the data point I underweighted going into this investigation. Ad revenue grew from ₹49 crore in FY24 to ₹1,636 crore in FY26 — a 33× increase in two years. As a percentage of GMV, it went from 1.11% to 7.78%. Amazon's advertising business (considered the most sophisticated retail ad platform in the world) operates at roughly 7–8% of GMV in mature markets.

I want to explain why this matters for the core question about discounts. If Zepto can sell advertising at 7–8% of GMV, the unit economics of the entire business change. A brand like Nestlé pays wholesale price for Maggi that Zepto sells at a discount. Nestlé accepts this because it gets volume. But then Nestlé pays again — for priority placement in Zepto's search results, for category banners, for promoted listings. That second payment is substantially higher-margin than the grocery transaction itself.

In this model, the grocery discount isn't waste. It's customer acquisition cost for the advertising platform. The question is whether this reframing holds at Zepto's scale, or whether it's a narrative that only works after a certain density of users and brands.

The advertising revenue trajectory is the most significant positive surprise in this investigation. The rate of growth from 1.1% to 7.8% of GMV in two years exceeds what I would have predicted. If this sustains, it materially improves the path to profitability — independent of whether Zepto reduces discounts.
What I'm still not sure about
Zepto's advertising is still much smaller in absolute terms than Blinkit's. As Blinkit pulls further ahead on market share, the better-performing ad inventory may shift toward Blinkit — brands pay for reach, and 46% market share commands more premium placement than 29%. If Zepto loses share, the advertising flywheel could slow precisely when it needs to accelerate.

The profitability trajectory is genuinely improving, and faster than I initially assumed. EBITDA loss per order fell from ₹136 in FY25 to ₹59 in Q4 FY26 — a 57% improvement in under a year. Total cost per order fell from ₹181 to ₹128 between Q2 and Q4 FY26 alone.

The mechanism behind this improvement is worth understanding. More orders per dark store per day means fixed costs are spread across more deliveries. Orders per store per day went from roughly 1,325 in FY24 to 2,140 in Q4 FY26. This is operating leverage — the cost structure doesn't scale linearly with order volume, so each incremental order improves margins.

The problem is the capital structure. Zepto's estimated cash position is around $700–900 million (per CEO statements and inferred from fundraising history). At the FY26 operating cash outflow rate of ₹3,462 crore, that gives roughly 18–24 months of runway before the IPO capital becomes necessary. The IPO is not just a milestone — it's a capital necessity.

The Blinkit comparison I found uncomfortable
Blinkit's adjusted EBITDA per order in FY26 was +₹3.02 — positive. Zepto's was –₹59. They operate in the same market, sell the same products, and use the same dark store model. The gap represents roughly 2–3 years of operational maturity. Blinkit also has ₹1.7 billion in cash versus Zepto's ~$700 million. In a prolonged discount war, Zepto faces this gap while holding less capital.

The counter-argument — which I think is legitimate — is that Blinkit had Zomato's cash and cross-subsidy to reach profitability, which Zepto cannot access. What Zepto achieved independently (reaching ₹22,624 crore in revenue while approaching per-order breakeven) may be structurally harder than what Blinkit accomplished with a listed parent funding its losses. Whether that makes Zepto's trajectory more impressive or more precarious depends on your view of the IPO.

Question 5

What conditions would make my entire thesis wrong?

Why I investigated this last
I think any analysis that only builds the case for its conclusion is incomplete. This section is an attempt to steelman the bear case — not as a counterpoint to my conclusions, but as a genuine stress test of whether I've missed something important.
High reliability The Nash equilibrium problem — no one can stop discounting first
+

This is the structural risk I find most credible. All three platforms — Zepto, Blinkit, Instamart — know that sustained discounting damages their unit economics. But none can unilaterally reduce subsidies without immediately ceding market share to the others. The result is what game theorists call a Nash equilibrium: everyone is behaving rationally given what competitors are doing, but the collective outcome (perpetual losses) is irrational.

The clearest evidence of this is the combined sector cash burn. Despite all three platforms collectively losing approximately ₹9,000 crore over a nine-to-eleven month period, no platform has meaningfully reduced promotional intensity. The March 2026 sequential user count decline at Zepto — despite spending ₹1,389 crore on advertising that year — suggests the discounts may be working less efficiently than before.

What would change this: A platform reaching sufficient market dominance that it could raise prices without losing meaningful share. Blinkit at 46% share is closest to this position. If Blinkit reaches 55%+ and begins reducing subsidies, it could force a sector-wide repricing. That benefits Blinkit, not Zepto.

Source: Moneycontrol sector analysis · Eternal Q4 FY26 disclosures · Zepto DRHP
High reliability FY25–26 cohort retention — the number that keeps me honest
+

I've mentioned the FY25 cohort retention figure (31.1%) throughout this investigation, but I want to be explicit here about what it would mean if this isn't a temporary cohort lag. If users acquired in FY25 and FY26 — who represent a large majority of Zepto's current user base — retain at only 31% rather than 47%, the LTV calculations change substantially. At 31% retention, the payback period on customer acquisition cost extends significantly, and the advertising revenue per user also declines.

The argument I've been giving this number the benefit of the doubt on is that newer geographies and user segments often take longer to form habits. FY23 users were primarily metro, tech-savvy, and relatively high-income. FY25–26 users include more Tier-2 city residents and more price-sensitive demographics. It's possible the retention curve looks the same — just shifted 6–8 quarters later.

But I cannot verify this. And if it's wrong, the entire habit formation thesis is weaker than I've concluded.

Source: Zepto DRHP · GrowthX analysis
Medium reliability Regulatory overhang — ED, CCPA, CCI, all concurrent
+

I initially dismissed the regulatory risks as standard-issue noise around a fast-growing startup. I revised this view slightly after reading the DRHP disclosures carefully. The ED summons to both co-founders in April 2026 under FEMA relates to foreign investment documentation — the DRHP discloses this explicitly. The CCPA issued a penalty for "dark patterns" (basket sneaking, drip pricing) that Zepto has appealed. CCI is examining predatory pricing.

None of these individually is likely fatal. Collectively, happening simultaneously immediately before an IPO, they represent a meaningful governance distraction. The SEBI eligibility disclosure — that Zepto doesn't meet standard listing criteria and is using a special growth-stage provision — is also worth noting for retail investors, though it doesn't change the underlying business analysis.

My reliability rating here is "medium" because I can't assess the probability or severity of these regulatory outcomes from public information alone.

Source: Zepto DRHP · SEBI regulations · Economic Times reporting
Lower reliability Amazon or Reliance enters quick commerce at scale
+

Flipkart Minutes has already entered. Amazon has launched quick commerce in India. Reliance's JioMart operates at significant scale. I've rated this lower reliability not because the risk isn't real, but because I don't have sufficient data on how aggressively these players will expand.

The structural risk is straightforward: if Reliance or Amazon enter quick commerce with their existing supply chain advantages and customer relationships, they can sustain higher subsidies for longer than Zepto, Blinkit, or Instamart. The dark store buildout would be the primary barrier, and both have the capital to close that gap quickly if they choose to.

My honest assessment is that I don't know enough about Reliance's or Amazon's strategic priorities in India right now to assign a reliable probability to this scenario. I've flagged it as a risk but not modelled it explicitly.

Source: Press reporting on Flipkart Minutes, Amazon Fresh India

Investment Verdict

Where I landed after five months of research — and what I still don't know

My initial assumption going in was that Zepto's discount strategy was a cash bonfire dressed up in startup language. I expected to find weak retention, minimal structural advantage, and a business that would contract sharply as capital constraints tightened.

The evidence changed parts of that view. The FY23 cohort retention of 45–49% is a real number, not a narrative. The 91% decline in digital marketing cost per order is audited and points in a consistent direction. The advertising revenue growing to 7.8% of GMV in two years is a concrete commercial achievement, not a projection.

What the evidence did not change: the FY25 cohort retention gap is real and unresolved. The capital structure is tight enough that the IPO isn't optional. Blinkit is ahead on both economics and cash reserves.

My conditional verdict

Zepto's discount strategy did create something beyond purchased growth — but whether it created sufficient durability to justify the capital cost depends on a data point that won't exist until 2027: whether FY25 cohorts follow the FY23 retention curve.

At the last private valuation of $7B, I would not invest without seeing that cohort data. At the IPO (if priced closer to $8–9B with public market liquidity), the risk-reward is more interesting — the advertising flywheel justifies a longer runway than the grocery P&L alone suggests.

I would invest if
FY25 cohort retention reaches 38%+ by Q3 FY27. Ad revenue crosses 10% of GMV. IPO priced with reasonable discount to intrinsic value.
I would not invest if
FY25 cohort retention stays at 31%. Blinkit exceeds 50% market share before IPO. ED/FEMA proceedings escalate materially.
